    A Role-Oriented Taxonomy for Multi-Agent Systems¶

    Abstract. I propose a lightweight, role-oriented taxonomy for long-running multi-agent operations systems. The taxonomy defines six roles by responsibility rather than by agent instance: Dispatcher, Builder, Runtime, Verifier, Researcher, and Archivist. I identify four cross-cutting concerns that constrain every role—memory, ownership, observability, and fallbacks—and map the patterns from a companion field study onto the roles they most affect. The taxonomy is intended as a design tool for practitioners and as a step toward more comparative research in multi-agent coordination.

    1. Introduction¶

    Multi-agent systems are often described by their number of agents, their model provider, or their conversation protocol. These descriptions tell us little about who is responsible for what. In a long-running operations fleet, what matters is not how many agents there are but whether the right responsibility is applied to the right problem at the right time.

    I operated a fleet organised around six stable roles. In my implementation each role was usually assigned to a distinct agent instance, but the taxonomy itself is about responsibilities, not process topology. This paper defines the six roles, identifies the concerns that cut across all of them, and maps observed coordination patterns to the roles they most affect.

    2. Related work¶

    Existing multi-agent frameworks such as AutoGen, CrewAI, and DSPy provide conversation structure, role-based crews, and declarative programming tools. They do not, however, prescribe how roles behave over months of operation, how they share persistent memory, or how they recover when a role is unavailable or wrong.

    Site reliability engineering and incident-management literature provides mature concepts of ownership, gates, post-mortems, and error budgets. My contribution is not to introduce those concepts but to adapt them to a setting where the operators are LLM agents with bounded context and tool access.

    3. Core roles¶

    Dispatcher¶

    Responsibility: decides who does what, monitors progress, resolves ambiguity.

    The Dispatcher is the only role whose primary job is coordination. It does not implement, verify, or operate directly; it routes tasks, detects stalls, and escalates when the fleet cannot resolve a blocker. A good Dispatcher asks, “Who owns this domain?” not “Who is free?”

    Example. A user reports that "the service is slow." The Dispatcher routes the task to the Runtime role for health and infrastructure diagnosis, not to the Builder role for code changes. Only after the Runtime role confirms the bottleneck is in code does the Dispatcher pull in the Builder.

    Key failure mode: confident misrouting, loops without progress.

    Builder¶

    Responsibility: implements, modifies, and packages code or configuration.

    The Builder turns decisions into artefacts. It writes patches, updates manifests, and produces the deliverables that other roles consume. Its work is most visible, which makes it a frequent target of misrouted tasks.

    Example. The Builder receives a verifiable dispatch: "Update the health-check endpoint so the fleet-wide liveness probe returns HTTP 200 within 2 seconds. Do not mark complete until the Verifier reports the probe green on the staging fleet." The Builder’s deliverable is the green/red verdict from the Verifier, not a status update.

    Key failure mode: being asked to fix runtime or verification problems outside its domain.

    Runtime¶

    Responsibility: operates infrastructure and keeps services alive.

    The Runtime role owns deploys, restarts, health checks, and incident response. It is closest to the live system and therefore the natural owner of secrets, stateful resources, and recovery playbooks.

    Example. After a compute environment is rebuilt, the Runtime role must ensure stateful resources point to the new environment identity before the Builder schedules workloads. If the identity hand-off is skipped, healthy-looking compute will fail to attach storage.

    Key failure mode: stale hand-offs after recovery, ungoverned secret mutation.

    Verifier¶

    Responsibility: checks correctness, runs tests, and audits outputs.

    The Verifier defines green/red criteria and posts verdicts. It is not a generic critic; it is a gatekeeper with written, phase-specific criteria that the fleet uses to advance work.

    Example. A migration has three phases: render manifests, apply to staging, apply to production. The Verifier declares the staging phase green only when the health endpoint reports HTTP 200 for five minutes. The Dispatcher will not schedule production until that verdict is posted.

    Key failure mode: treating per-step tool success as mission success without an end-to-end gate.

    Researcher¶

    Responsibility: gathers information, evaluates options, and reduces uncertainty.

    The Researcher role reduces unknowns before the Dispatcher commits the fleet to a path. It evaluates alternatives, finds precedents, and flags risks. It does not own implementation or verification.

    Example. Before choosing a secret-rotation strategy, the Researcher compares three approaches, notes that one requires downtime, and produces a decision artefact: "Option C is safest because it keeps rotation and re-seeding explicit." The Dispatcher then routes implementation to the Runtime and Builder roles.

    Key failure mode: over-researching without producing a decision artefact, or being routed implementation work.

    Archivist¶

    Responsibility: maintains shared memory, context, continuity, and agent guidance.

    The Archivist role decides what is remembered, how it is retrieved, and who can read or write it. It owns the shared-memory contract: identity, namespaces, promotion rules, and skill libraries.

    Example. The Archivist role maintains the skill library. When two profiles contain conflicting copies of the same triage skill, the Archivist retires the duplicate, keeps one canonical copy with owner metadata, and updates the guidance so agents reject untagged skills.

    Key failure mode: drifting context, silent dependency breaks, surface-level drift in the guidance library.

    4. Cross-cutting concerns¶

    Four concerns affect every role:

    • Memory. Who reads, who writes, what is canonical, and how identity is preserved across sessions. Without explicit answers, agents write into incompatible buckets or lose context.
    • Ownership. Which role is accountable when a hand-off fails. Clear ownership prevents blame diffusion and makes misrouting auditable.
    • Observability. How the fleet knows its own state. Every role must be able to report a status that another role can verify.
    • Fallbacks. What happens when a role is unavailable, wrong, or overloaded. Fallbacks prevent confident misrouting from becoming a hard failure.

    Example. A Dispatcher is unavailable. Because ownership and fallbacks are explicit, the fleet knows the Builder can still execute pre-approved verifiable tasks and escalate only on new blockers, rather than stall or guess.

    5. Role-to-pattern mapping¶

    The patterns from the companion field study map to roles as follows:

    Pattern / Anti-pattern Primary role Cross-cutting concern
    Role-based routing Dispatcher Ownership
    Verifiable dispatch instruction Dispatcher Observability
    Status-only check-ins Dispatcher Observability
    Confident misrouting Dispatcher Ownership, Fallbacks
    Externalise session state Archivist Memory
    Explicit identity in shared tools Archivist Memory, Ownership
    Local-first, shared-when-useful Archivist Memory
    Drifting context Archivist Memory
    Silent dependency break Archivist Memory, Ownership
    Gate before forward motion Verifier Observability
    Calibrate evaluation metrics Verifier Observability
    Tool success, mission failure Verifier Observability
    Surface-level drift Archivist Memory, Ownership
    Coupled urgent-fix bundles ship together Dispatcher, Runtime Ownership
    Idempotent secret bootstrap Runtime Ownership
    Render the desired state Runtime Memory, Ownership
    Ungoverned secret mutation Runtime Ownership, Memory
    Stale hand-off Runtime Memory, Ownership
    Single-owner skills and docs Archivist Ownership, Memory
    Drive forward without permission stalls Dispatcher Observability, Fallbacks
    Post-mission review as ritual All Observability
    Decisions without landing rituals Dispatcher Ownership

    6. Discussion¶

    The taxonomy suggests that reliability in a multi-agent fleet comes from designing roles before designing agents. A common mistake is to start with a set of model instances and then decide what each should do. A more robust approach is to define the responsibilities, the contracts between them, and the fallbacks, and only then map roles to runtime instances.

    Example. A team starts with three generic agent instances and assigns tasks ad hoc. Soon the same manifest is edited by two agents, a secret is rotated by a third, and no one knows who should verify the result. Applying the taxonomy first would have assigned ownership of the manifest to the Builder, the secret to the Runtime, and the verification gate to the Verifier.

    The six roles here are not claimed to be exhaustive. Other fleets may need a human-facing liaison, a security role, or a compliance role. The taxonomy is offered as a starting vocabulary and as a way to make coordination assumptions explicit.

    7. Limitations and future work¶

    This taxonomy is derived from one fleet. Other systems may organise responsibilities differently. Future work could compare this taxonomy to AutoGen, CrewAI, and DSPy roles, and could evaluate whether adding or splitting roles improves outcomes. I pursue an evaluation protocol for persistent multi-agent workflows in a companion paper.

    8. Conclusion¶

    I proposed a role-oriented taxonomy with six responsibilities—Dispatcher, Builder, Runtime, Verifier, Researcher, and Archivist—and four cross-cutting concerns: memory, ownership, observability, and fallbacks. Mapping companion field-study patterns onto this taxonomy shows that most failures and most effective strategies concern the contracts between roles rather than the capability of any single agent. I offer this taxonomy as a design tool for practitioners and as groundwork for comparative research in multi-agent coordination.
